Datos Health is a customizable remote-care platform for healthcare systems that want to move monitoring, education, reminders, triage, and virtual touchpoints into one workflow. Their core buyer appears to be clinical teams and health-system operators running remote patient monitoring or hospital-at-home programs, especially for higher-acuity patients after discharge.

Mission & the “Why”

Datos Health’s mission is to replace rigid RPM point solutions with an open platform that lets clinicians design and deploy personalized remote care workflows quickly, without heavy IT involvement. The problem they’re solving is fragmentation: multiple devices, protocols, alerts, patient education, and follow-up tasks are usually spread across disconnected tools, which increases clinician workload and reduces adherence. The primary persona is likely a care-program owner or clinical operations leader at a hospital or health system who needs to scale hybrid care while preserving clinical control.

Product Matrix

Core Product: Open Care platform; no-code Design Studio; patient CareApps; workflow automation; device-agnostic RPM integration; Care Team Dashboard.

Adjacent Features: Biometric and PROM collection; educational content delivery; medication reminders; questionnaires; two-way communication; virtual visits; virtual rounding; automated assisted self-care.

Upsell / Enterprise tiers: Turnkey hospital-at-home programs; device provisioning and logistics support; training and support for clinical/admin teams; EMR integration; reimbursement support for RPM CPT workflows; custom protocol build-outs.

Industry Terms & Mental Models

Open Care: Their umbrella category for a flexible, clinician-configurable remote-care platform, meant to contrast with “rigid” RPM tools.

CareApps: Their label for the patient-facing app experiences generated from workflows, implying a care-specific app rather than a generic portal.

Design Studio: A drag-and-drop builder that frames care design like product design, reducing dependency on engineering.

Automated assisted self-care: A behavioral model where the patient is guided step-by-step, but the system automates much of the follow-up and escalation.

Hospital at home / HiTH: Their category for high-acuity remote care after discharge, positioning them beyond basic RPM.

Care Team Dashboard: The clinician console for monitoring trends, alerts, and interventions, which centers the workflow on operations rather than raw data.

RPA engine: They borrow automation language from enterprise software to signal that clinical workflows can be encoded and executed automatically.

PM Edge Analysis

Their USP is not just remote monitoring; it is configurable hybrid-care orchestration across any clinical domain with device agnosticism, no-code workflow design, and automation that turns a protocol into a patient-facing experience quickly. Compared with more prebuilt RPM vendors, they emphasize “your care, your way,” which is a strong wedge for health systems that want standardization without losing local clinical nuance. Their monetization appears to be enterprise SaaS with implementation/support services, likely packaged around subscriptions plus onboarding and optional turnkey services; public pricing is not disclosed on the site, but third-party listings describe subscription-style pricing and free trial/version references.

Likely Friction & Gaps

The marketing suggests a strong automation story, but the likely pain points are integration complexity, protocol governance, and clinician trust in automated escalation logic. They also appear to rely heavily on health-system operational maturity: if a customer lacks clear care pathways, the platform’s flexibility may feel like extra work rather than simplicity. Another likely gap is proof of outcome differentiation by specialty, since the site speaks broadly across use cases rather than showing deeply quantified results for each clinical vertical.

HiTH product analysis

Datos positions HiTH as post-discharge programs that typically run for about 12 weeks, using biometrics, PROMs, medication adherence, education, reminders, virtual visits, and virtual rounding to replicate hospital oversight at home. The product is not just “monitor vitals”; it is a protocol orchestration layer where clinicians decide which measurements matter, when alerts fire, and which steps are automated. That means the real product is a managed clinical workflow, not a standalone monitoring app.

What HiTH is optimized for

The strongest use case is reducing readmissions while keeping high-acuity patients out of the hospital after discharge. Datos leans hard into operational benefits: fewer manual tasks, earlier intervention, less alert fatigue through device integration and scoring, and easier continuity across departments. The presence of turnkey options for device provisioning and logistics also suggests they target health systems that want a more complete operational package, not just software.

Mobile app analysis

The Datos mobile app is the patient-side interface for the HiTH engine and broader RPM workflows. Its core job is to let patients connect devices, submit measurements, receive guidance and encouragement, message the care team, and see that their data is shared back to clinicians. The app also supports Apple Health integration for some wearables, which lowers friction by letting patients reuse existing devices instead of forcing new hardware adoption.

App UX and behavioral design

The app appears designed around engagement and adherence rather than complexity. The language in the store listing emphasizes “take charge,” “stay motivated,” and “personalized guidance,” which shows a self-management framing instead of a passive patient portal. The newest update notes conversation resolution and invalidating measurements, which implies the product is evolving toward a more structured care-communication workflow, not just data collection. Its multilingual support, free availability, and low iOS requirement also signal a broad accessibility strategy.

PM read on the system

The combined system creates a clear operating model: clinicians define a care pathway in the platform, Datos automates the routine steps, and the mobile app keeps the patient compliant and connected. Their edge is that HiTH is not presented as a generic telehealth feature; it is a packaged, high-acuity care model with automation, device flexibility, and workflow customization built in. The likely tradeoff is that this level of flexibility may require stronger implementation support and clinical design effort than a simpler out-of-the-box RPM product.
